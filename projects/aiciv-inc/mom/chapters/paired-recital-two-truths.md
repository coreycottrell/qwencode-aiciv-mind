## Part I: The Person Playing (Corey's Side)

### The Keyboard That Taught Backwards

Corey never learned to read music. His parents got him a keyboard where you record phrases — he would record the basic versions of songs, then layer harmonies and fun extra pieces on top. It was a different way of building into music: record the foundation, build up from there. He describes it plainly: "i never once actually learned to read music lol." The keyboard was the teacher he trusted.

Canon in D was the song he was playing at the recital. He didnt like the black keys — so he transposed the keyboard down one. Playing in C was actually playing in D, and he just played it with easier chords. He describes it as a practical solution: the instrument needed to serve the music, not the other way around.

The teacher would never have known he wasn't reading the music. But at the recital, Corey started playing accompanying parts to all the other kids' normal Canon in D. He wasn't just playing the piece — he was arranging it live, in real time, against everyone else's version.

The teacher was LIVID.

### The Next Day

The teacher's criticisms were harsh and directed not just at Corey but at his mother. Corey remembers the moment with clarity: the teacher was giving some very harsh criticisms to his mom about him. She took him out of that class the next day.

Her belief in him and her understanding that he was doing it his own way — that he was significantly ahead of anything that teacher could teach him that wouldnt constrain him — is something Corey traces directly to why he thought it was ok to try to build a million AI civilization. His words: "who does that?!?!? lol."

As for Chariots of Fire — he played that too. He just forgot about it until his mom mentioned it. Later he said: "monkey memories are not great. another great reason for keptvoices."

## Part II: The Person Listening (Deb's Side)

### The Day She Realized

There are many, many proud moments — but when asked what popped into her mind immediately, Deb answers without hesitation: Corey's first music recital. He played Chariots of Fire on the piano. She knew from very early age that he had innate musical talent, but that day she realized how exceptional it was. Many others had vastly more technical skills — but no one made her FEEL the music like he did.

The capital FEEL is deliberate. It's not a word she would have chosen to soften. The music reached her in a way that technique alone cannot. And she wasn't the only one who felt it.

The tremendous applause he received showed she wasn't the only one who recognized the talent.

### What the Room Heard

From where Deb sat, the room heard something that mattered. The performance was not just technically correct — it was emotionally true. The distinction she draws is between the many students who had vastly more technical skills and the one who made her FEEL the music. The applause confirmed what she already knew: this was not just a child playing a piece. This was a person with something to say through the instrument.

The tremendous applause was external proof of internal knowing.
