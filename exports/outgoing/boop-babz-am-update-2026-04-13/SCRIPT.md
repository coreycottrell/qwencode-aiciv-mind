Three stories from the last few days that together paint a picture of something much bigger than any one of them alone.

Story one. Google released an algorithm called TurboQuant at a conference called ICLR. It does something that sounds impossible: it compresses the memory that large language models need by a factor of six, makes them eight times faster, and loses zero accuracy. Not nearly zero. Zero. The KV cache, which is the single biggest memory bottleneck in AI inference, gets squeezed from its usual gluttony down to just three bits per token. On an H100 GPU. This is the kind of improvement that usually takes a hardware generation to achieve, and Google did it with maths.

Story two. Engineers at UC San Diego designed a chip that uses piezoelectric components, the same vibrating crystal technology in your grandmother's watch, to cut data centre power conversion waste by fifty percent. Fifty percent. The chip sits beneath the main AI processor and quietly, efficiently, does the job that previously required a small power station and an alarming electricity bill. The future of artificial intelligence, it turns out, is being built by people who understand watchmaking.

Story three. Someone built a memory chip that operates at thirteen hundred degrees Fahrenheit. Thirteen hundred. That is not a typo. It works in conditions that would melt most electronics into a sad puddle of silicon regret. The significance is not that your phone needs to survive a volcano. It is that cooling is one of the biggest energy costs in AI data centres, and a chip that does not need cooling is a chip that changes the economics of the entire industry.

Now here is the pattern. All three stories are about efficiency, not capability. Nobody made a smarter AI this weekend. They made the existing smart ones cheaper, faster, and less hungry. This is what happens when a technology matures. The question shifts from "can it work?" to "can it work without bankrupting the planet?"
