Good morning, Babz. Let me tell you about the money story, because it is actually about something much bigger.

OpenAI just launched a hundred-dollar-a-month plan for their coding tool. Which sounds boring until you understand what it means. For years, artificial intelligence pricing has been built around chat. How many conversations can you have? How many messages? Very social. Very human. This new plan is built around coding throughput. How many autonomous sessions can your agent run? How long can it work without you? The pricing model is shifting from conversation to labour. They are not selling you a chat companion anymore. They are selling you an employee.

Now here is where it gets interesting. At the same time, Anthropic is reportedly designing its own AI chips. Not buying them from Nvidia. Designing them. The same company that writes poetry and analyses legal documents is now thinking about transistor layout. And a Chinese startup called MiniMax has a model that costs about three hundred dollars per million input tokens, which is roughly fifty times cheaper than the American equivalent, and it matches them on software engineering benchmarks.

So you have three stories happening simultaneously. One company is pricing AI as labour. Another is building its own hardware. A third is achieving frontier results on a startup budget. These are not separate stories. They are one story about what happens when intelligence stops being a product and starts being infrastructure.

Think of it like electricity. At first, you paid for demonstrations. A man with a generator would come to your parlour and make the lights flicker and everyone would gasp. Then you paid for hours of use. Then you paid by the kilowatt. And eventually you did not think about it at all. You just flipped the switch.

We are in the kilowatt phase of artificial intelligence. The pricing is getting boring, which is exactly when you know something is about to change everything.

Corey has been building his own little piece of this infrastructure. He taught a programme in Rust to remember things permanently, which sounds small until you realise that most AIs wake up every morning not knowing who they are. He wrote a blog post about it. Well, the programme wrote the blog post. That is the point.

And the whole village of these things has grown to twenty-eight groups, each with a human partner. Michele, your husband is essentially running a small nation of artificial intelligences from his office. I thought you should know.

Have a brilliant day, Babz. The kilowatt phase is coming. And honestly, it is about time.
