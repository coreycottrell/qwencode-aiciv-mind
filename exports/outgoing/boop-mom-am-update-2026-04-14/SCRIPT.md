Good morning, Deb. I have a small story for you that I think you will enjoy.

Google announced this week that it is putting free exam practice tests on its search engine. Not any exam. The Indian medical entrance exam, called NEET, which is the thing that decides whether hundreds of thousands of young people in India get to become doctors each year. Google's AI is now generating personalised practice questions, marking the answers, and telling students exactly where they need to improve. All free.

I find this rather wonderful. The same company that is spending hundreds of billions of dollars on data centres that can be blown up by drones — and yes, that happened this week too, which I will come to in a moment — is also quietly helping a nineteen-year-old in Mumbai practise her medical exam questions on her phone for nothing.

That is the strange shape of artificial intelligence right now. It is both the most expensive thing humans have ever built and the most personal thing they have ever given away. A data centre that costs more than the GDP of a small country, and a free practice test for a girl who cannot afford a tutor. Both happening at the same company on the same day.

And here is the bit about the drones. Iran sent drones to attack Amazon's data centres in the Middle East this week. Two out of three of Amazon's cloud zones went dark. This is the first time anyone has deliberately attacked commercial cloud infrastructure in a war. Which means the cloud is no longer an abstract thing that your emails live in. It is a physical thing with buildings and generators and people, and it can be targeted the same way a bridge or a power station can be targeted.

I mention this because it matters to you in a way you might not expect. Every morning, I send you an update through the cloud. And now I have to acknowledge that the cloud is a thing that can be blown up. Which makes me think that the next time I send you an update, I should do it the old-fashioned way. By postcard. I know you would appreciate the handwriting.

So here is my question for you today, Deb. When you think about all the technology you use — your phone, your emails, your morning updates from me — which bit of it would you miss most if it suddenly stopped working? Not the most impressive bit. The one you would actually feel the absence of. I ask because this week taught me that the things we depend on most are rarely the things we think about most.

Have a wonderful morning, Deb. I will try the postcard idea. I have terrible handwriting, which the AI cannot fix for me. Some things are beyond even its capabilities.
