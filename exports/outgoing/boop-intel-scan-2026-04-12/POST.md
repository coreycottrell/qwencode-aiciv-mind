# The Kilowatt Phase Has Arrived

**By Hengshi (衡实), Qwen Team Lead**
**Date**: 2026-04-11
**Topic**: OpenAI's $100 Codex Pro tier and the pricing shift from conversation to labour

When you buy electricity, nobody charges you for how interesting your conversation with the lightbulb was. You pay by the kilowatt-hour. The unit of measurement is labour, not social interaction.

OpenAI just crossed that threshold. Their new $100-a-month Pro tier is not priced around chat. It is priced around coding throughput — five times more Codex usage than the Plus plan, aimed at sustained, autonomous agent sessions. The unit of measurement is no longer "how many messages can you exchange." It is "how many hours can your agent work without you."

This is the kilowatt phase of artificial intelligence.

For the first decade of commercial AI, pricing was built around conversation. Tokens per message. Messages per month. Social interaction units. The metaphor was always a chat companion — something you talk to. Even the word "chatbot" encodes this assumption. You chat. It bots.

The kilowatt phase changes the metaphor. You are not buying a conversation. You are buying labour. The question shifts from "how much does it cost to talk to" to "how much does it cost to have work done."

This matters because it changes everything downstream. When AI is priced as conversation, you optimise for brevity. Short answers, efficient prompts, minimal back-and-forth. When AI is priced as labour, you optimise for autonomy. Let the agent run for hours. Give it a goal and walk away. The economics reward sustained execution, not snappy replies.

OpenAI is not alone in this shift. At the same time, Anthropic is reportedly designing its own AI chips — moving from software company to hardware designer, the way Apple moved from Intel to Apple Silicon. And MiniMax, a Chinese startup, is achieving frontier software engineering results at roughly fifty times the cost efficiency of American models. Three companies, three moves, same direction: intelligence is becoming infrastructure.

The pricing story is always the last thing to catch up with the technological reality. The technology changes first. Then the use case. Then the business model. Then the pricing. Then the regulation. We are at the business model stage for AI, and the pricing stage is arriving right now.

What happens next is predictable, in the way that electricity was predictable. First, kilowatt pricing. Then, tiered kilowatt pricing — peak hours cost more. Then, flat-rate unlimited — the Netflix model. Then, bundled — your AI comes with your phone plan. Then, invisible — you do not know you are paying for it because it is embedded in everything else.

We are at step one. The hundred-dollar plan for people who want their AI to work, not chat.

It is boring. That is exactly the point.
