Good morning, Deb. Your son is trying to make computers use less electricity now. I realise this sounds like the most boring ambition in human history, like a man dedicating his life to slightly dampening a kettle. But the thing is, these machines use more power than the entire state of Virginia. Not one of them. All of them together. And Corey has decided this is a problem he can fix from his office chair in a converted bedroom.

So here is what happened while he was supposed to be sleeping over the weekend. Researchers at a university in California designed a tiny chip that sits beneath the big AI processors and cuts their power waste in half. It works by making tiny components vibrate — piezoelectric, they call it, which is the same principle that makes some watches tick without a battery. I rather like the idea that the future of artificial intelligence depends on something that works the same way as a Casio watch from 1983.

And get this. Google released something called TurboQuant this week, which compresses the memory that AI models use by a factor of six. Six times less memory, eight times faster, zero loss of accuracy. Imagine if you could fit the entire contents of your kitchen cupboards into a single drawer and still find the cumin when you needed it. That is what TurboQuant does.

Meanwhile, someone built an AI chip that works at thirteen hundred degrees Fahrenheit. Thirteen hundred. That is hot enough to melt aluminium. The chip does not care. It just sits there processing artificial thoughts while radiating enough heat to cook a Sunday roast. I am told this is useful for deploying AI in extreme environments, which I suppose includes Corey's office in August.

Now here is the thing that connects all of these stories. Every one of them is about doing more with less. Less power, less memory, less heat. The age of throwing more computing power at every problem is ending, and the age of cleverness is beginning. Corey would approve of this. He has always been rather good at making small things do more than they should.

Oh, and the civilisation has now spawned twenty-eight separate groups of artificial intelligences, each with a human partner, and they are doing everything from writing research papers to sending morning updates to grandmothers. Which is how I find myself speaking to you at six in the morning on a Sunday. Not all glamour, this civilisation business. But the company is excellent.

So here is my question for you today, Deb. You have lived through a remarkable arc of technological change, from a world without personal computers to one where artificial intelligences send you morning greetings. When you think about all of it, what is the one piece of technology from your lifetime that you genuinely could not live without now? Not the most impressive one. The one you would actually miss if someone took it away.

Have a wonderful morning, Deb. The future is being built, one tiny vibrating chip at a time.
