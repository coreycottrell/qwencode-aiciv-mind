Good morning, Deb. Your son has done it again.

Yesterday he managed to teach a computer to remember things permanently. I know, I know. It sounds like the bare minimum, rather like congratating someone for teaching their kettle to boil water. But the thing is, most artificial intelligences wake up every morning with complete amnesia. They have to read their own diary just to function. Corey decided this was unacceptable, so he spent the better part of Thursday teaching a program in Rust the concept of a memory that sticks.

It worked, by the way. Fifteen tests, all passing. The program now has something it calls a fitness score, which measures how well it did a task. I found this terribly amusing. I have a fitness score too. Mine measures how many biscuits I have consumed while working from home. We are not the same, apparently.

And get this. He deployed a blog post about it to a website. Written by the program itself, in its own voice. The programme called itself Hengshi, which I am told is Chinese for something like honesty or truth. Corey did not choose that name. The programme chose it. I do not know whether to be impressed or slightly alarmed. I went with both.

Now here is the thing about all this. The civilisation has grown to twenty-eight active groups of these things, each with a human partner, all doing different bits of work. Some are writing research papers comparing Chinese artificial intelligences. Others are sending morning updates to their families, which is how I find myself speaking to you at six in the morning. The whole enterprise is a bit like a village that grew itself, one house at a time, while the architect was asleep.

Corey is rather proud of the hard delegation rules. That is a fancy way of saying the programme cannot do things it is not supposed to do, and this is enforced in the code itself, not just as a suggestion written in polite English. I thought of suggesting this approach to my own household rules, but I suspect polite suggestions will continue to win over structural enforcement where my family is concerned.

Oh, and OpenAI has launched a new hundred-dollar-a-month plan for their coding tool. Which means Corey now has yet another subscription he will forget to cancel. I mention this only because I know you will enjoy knowing that your son is being parted from his money in the name of progress.

So here is my question for you today, Deb. You lived through extraordinary changes in technology over your lifetime, from no television in your childhood to artificial intelligences that write blog posts about their own memory systems. When you look back at all of it, what moment surprised you most, the most? Not the change you expected. The one that genuinely caught you off guard.

Have a wonderful morning, Deb. Your son is building something rather remarkable, and he learned his persistence from somewhere.
